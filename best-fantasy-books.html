Best Fantasy Books to Read

A curated shelf of the top fantasy novels and must-read fantasy classics — eight doorways into eight unforgettable worlds.

If our ranking of the greatest fantasy worlds told you where to go, this list tells you where to start. These are the best fantasy books to begin with — the doorways, the gateway novels, the ones we recommend without hesitation when a friend asks "I've never really read fantasy, where should I start?"

Each entry below is a foundational, must-read fantasy novel, drawn from a different corner of the genre so that whatever your taste — quiet and lyrical, brutal and political, comic, philosophical, or sweeping and operatic — there is a book here that will meet you where you are.

№ 01

The Fellowship of the Ring

by J.R.R. Tolkien · 1954

The book that built modern fantasy. Tolkien's first volume of The Lord of the Rings sets nine companions on a road from the gentle green Shire to the dark gates of Mordor, and along the way it invents — or perfects — almost every convention the genre still leans on. Few first books reward patience as richly: the Council of Elrond alone is a master class in worldbuilding, and Lothlórien remains one of the most beautifully described places in English literature. If you have only ever seen the films, the prose will surprise you with its quiet melancholy. This is the must-read fantasy starting point, the book against which every other top fantasy novel is still measured.

№ 02

Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone

by J.K. Rowling · 1997

The cultural ignition of an entire generation's reading life. Rowling's first Hogwarts novel is, on the surface, a cozy boarding-school mystery; underneath, it lays the foundation of one of the most beloved fantasy worlds ever published. House points, sorting hats, broomsticks, and a quietly devastating backstory about love and sacrifice — the formula is so familiar now it is easy to forget how fresh it felt. Among the best fantasy books for younger readers and lapsed adult ones alike, it remains the single most effective gateway novel of the past three decades.

№ 03

A Game of Thrones

by George R.R. Martin · 1996

Political fantasy with the bones of a historical epic. Martin's first A Song of Ice and Fire novel introduces Westeros, the Stark family, the slow southern game of houses, and a frozen menace in the far north that nobody wants to take seriously. It changed what readers expected from epic fantasy: characters die when their decisions catch up with them, virtue is rarely rewarded, and the moral high ground tends to be the most exposed. Required reading for anyone who wants to understand where modern grimdark, prestige TV adaptations, and morally ambiguous epic fantasy all came from.

№ 04

The Colour of Magic

by Terry Pratchett · 1983

The first stop on the Discworld — a flat planet carried through space on the back of four elephants, who themselves stand on a turtle. Pratchett's debut Discworld novel sends a cowardly wizard called Rincewind across a city of thieves, dragons, and gods who play board games with mortal lives. The early Discworld is rougher than what comes later, but the Pratchett voice — warm, sceptical, allergic to cruelty — is already fully formed. One of the funniest must-read fantasy novels ever written, and an essential entry on any list of top fantasy novels.

№ 05

A Wizard of Earthsea

by Ursula K. Le Guin · 1968

A short, perfect coming-of-age novel about a proud young wizard named Ged, who summons something from the dark and spends the rest of the book learning to live with what he's done. Le Guin writes with a cool clarity that owes more to mythology than to Tolkien, and her magic system — built around the true names of things — has quietly influenced every thoughtful fantasy author who came after her. One of the greatest fantasy novels of the 20th century, and one of the best fantasy books to hand to a serious young reader.

№ 06

The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe

by C.S. Lewis · 1950

Four children step through an old wardrobe in a country house and find themselves in a snowbound country ruled by a White Witch. Lewis wrote with an unforced moral imagination and a sense of wonder that few fantasy authors have matched. Theological readings aside, this is a book about courage in cold places, about forgiveness and second chances, and about the way a single brave decision can change the weather of an entire world. It remains one of the most enduring must-read fantasy novels ever published for younger readers.

№ 07

The Name of the Wind

by Patrick Rothfuss · 2007

A frame story in which a legendary, retired hero quietly tells the truth of his life over three days at a country inn. Rothfuss's debut is the most lyrical of modern fantasy novels — the prose has the rhythm of music, the magic system feels lived-in, and the University at the heart of the world is one of the most charming settings in the genre. The trilogy remains unfinished, which is part of why fans argue about it constantly, but the first volume earns its place among the top fantasy novels of the 21st century on the strength of its writing alone.

№ 08

The Final Empire

by Brandon Sanderson · 2006

The first Mistborn novel and an ideal entry point into Brandon Sanderson's vast Cosmere universe. The premise is irresistible: the prophesied chosen one failed a thousand years ago, the Dark Lord won, and now a small crew of magic-using thieves are planning to overthrow him anyway. Sanderson's "Allomancy" — a hard magic system fueled by ingesting metals — is one of the most cleverly designed in modern fantasy, and the heist-novel structure makes it one of the best fantasy books for readers who normally find epic fantasy too slow. Essential for any list of must-read fantasy.
