Best Enemies to Lovers Books

A curated shelf of the most addictive enemies to lovers books and slow burn romance novels — sworn rivals, reluctant allies, and hatred that turns into something far more dangerous.

Of all the romantic tropes, none holds the reader quite like enemies to lovers. There is a particular electricity in a story where two characters begin in opposition — across battle lines, courtrooms, magical academies, or family feuds — and slowly, painfully, inevitably realize that the person they swore to defeat is the one person they cannot live without. The best enemies to lovers books understand that loathing and longing are often closer cousins than either side wants to admit.

This reading list collects some of the best romance fantasy books and slow burn romance books that have made the trope famous. If you've already worked through our best fantasy books shelf and our ranking of the greatest fantasy worlds, consider this the next door to step through. Each title below earns its place by doing the trope properly: the rivalry is real, the tension simmers across hundreds of pages, and the eventual surrender is fully, dazzlingly earned.

№ 01

Pride and Prejudice

by Jane Austen · 1813

The grandmother of every enemies to lovers book ever written. Elizabeth Bennet and Fitzwilliam Darcy meet badly, judge each other harder, and spend the rest of the novel slowly, mortifyingly discovering how wrong each was about the other. Austen's wit is the engine: every sharp exchange between them is built on real intelligence, real misjudgment, and real growth. What makes it compelling isn't the eventual proposal — it's the long, painful process of two proud people learning to see clearly. Two centuries on, this is still the single best slow burn romance ever written, and the template against which every modern enemies to lovers book is quietly measured.

№ 02

The Cruel Prince

by Holly Black · 2018

Jude is a mortal raised in the courts of Faerie. Cardan is the cruelest prince in those courts, who has tormented her since childhood. Their loathing is not metaphorical: there are humiliations, schemes, and at least one very good slap. And yet Holly Black builds the rivalry so carefully — gift by gift, betrayal by betrayal — that by the time the dynamic begins to shift, you understand exactly why neither of them can simply walk away. Among modern best romance fantasy books, this trilogy is the gold standard of fae-court enemies to lovers: morally murky, politically vicious, and genuinely surprising about who is using whom.

№ 03

The Hating Game

by Sally Thorne · 2016

Lucy and Joshua share an executive assistant office at a publishing company that has just merged. They cannot stand each other. They have an entire taxonomy of staring games, mirror games, and door-slamming protocols. Then a single promotion goes up for grabs, and everything escalates. Thorne's debut is one of the funniest, most readable contemporary enemies to lovers books on the shelf, and it absolutely understands the trope: the petty workplace warfare is real, the chemistry is undeniable, and the reveal of what Joshua actually thinks of Lucy is one of the most satisfying turns in modern romance writing. A perfect entry point if you're new to slow burn romance books.

№ 04

A Court of Mist and Fury

by Sarah J. Maas · 2016

Technically the second book in Maas's series, but the one where the trope truly arrives. Feyre begins this novel hating the High Lord of the Night Court for what she believes he did to her in the previous book. What unfolds across hundreds of pages is one of the most beloved slow burn romance arcs in modern fantasy: rivalry softening into wary respect, respect into trust, trust into something neither character is brave enough to name for half the book. It's one of the most-recommended best romance fantasy books of the last decade for good reason — the world is lush, the political stakes are real, and the eventual payoff is patient and earned.

№ 05

Beach Read

by Emily Henry · 2020

January writes hopeful romances. Augustus writes bleak literary fiction. They went to college together, hated each other, and now they are stuck in neighboring beach houses for a summer, both with crippling writer's block. They make a bet: she'll write something dark, he'll write something hopeful, and they'll teach each other how. Henry's strength is that the rivalry is grown-up — these are adults with histories, grief, and unresolved damage — and the slow burn romance unfolds through long honest conversations rather than melodrama. Genuinely one of the best contemporary enemies to lovers books, and a quieter, more emotionally precise entry on this list.

№ 06

From Blood and Ash

by Jennifer L. Armentrout · 2020

Poppy is the Maiden, raised in seclusion to fulfill a holy duty she doesn't fully understand. Hawke is her new royal guard, charming, infuriating, and almost certainly hiding something. The early enmity here is structural — duty against desire, faith against doubt — and Armentrout draws it out with skill, escalating each charged scene before pulling back again. It is one of the most binge-read best romance fantasy books of recent years, beloved for its banter and for the way the central rivalry gradually unravels into something entirely different. A compulsive recommendation for anyone hunting for a long, immersive slow burn romance series.

№ 07

Red, White & Royal Blue

by Casey McQuiston · 2019

Alex Claremont-Diaz is the First Son of the United States. Prince Henry of Wales is, in Alex's professional opinion, the world's most insufferable royal. After a tabloid-baiting incident at a royal wedding, the two are forced into a fake friendship for diplomatic damage control — which means a great deal of texting, a great deal of proximity, and a slow, hilarious recalibration of what each of them actually thought about the other. McQuiston's debut is one of the warmest, sharpest contemporary enemies to lovers books on the shelf, and one of the few in this genre that lets the political stakes feel as real as the romantic ones.

№ 08

Throne of Glass

by Sarah J. Maas · 2012

Celaena Sardothien is a teenage assassin pulled out of a salt mine to compete in a royal tournament. Chaol Westfall is the captain of the king's guard, ordered to keep her alive and resentful of every minute he has to spend doing it. The tension here is more rivalry-to-allies-to-something-more, the long arc of two people who begin on opposite sides of a corrupt regime and slowly find themselves trusting each other against it. As an entry into best romance fantasy books for younger adult readers, this series remains essential — and the early enemies to lovers dynamic is one of the things that built its devoted fanbase.
